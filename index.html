COURTYARD BIOME

PROJECT INTRODUCTION

During a year of living above an unremarkable courtyard, I started documenting how its structures, objects, and vegetation shifted over time. Over 50 days of observation — including a continuous 29-day sequence — the space revealed patterns of circulation, decay, accumulation, and growth. The courtyard behaved less like a location and more like a slow organism shaped by work, water, sunlight, tools, and habit.

This project treats the courtyard as a biome — something that can be observed, described, and anatomically interpreted.

It is built as a collaborative interpretation: I contributed the field material — layered, incomplete documentation of the courtyard — while artificial intelligence assisted in analysis, reconstruction, and speculative anatomy. Together, human observation and machine inference form a symbiotic representation of this biome.

Spaces are not still. They metabolize time.

About the Project

On August 29, 2024, I moved into a small rented apartment in Batumi, Georgia. Its windows faced not the sea, but a narrow courtyard trapped between tall buildings — improvised, unattractive, and seemingly not meant to be observed. Yet it was impossible not to look. I photographed it for the first time on August 31, reaching my phone through the fence to see what my eyes could not.

At first, the courtyard felt accidental and exposed, something to hide or apologize for. Over time, it began acting less like a “backyard” and more like a working physiology. People cooked there, stored tools, dried textiles, left objects exactly where they needed them. Vegetation crept across surfaces. Water evaporated and returned, leaving mineral scars. It was not a space of decoration. It was a space of metabolism.

Between 2024 and 2025, I photographed the courtyard on fifty separate days, including a continuous 29-day sequence. As the documentation continued, my role shifted from neighbor to observer, and eventually to something closer to a biologist. The courtyard stopped behaving like a background and began to function as a living biome — with organs, tissues, memory, waste, growth, decay, and repair.

What the Courtyard Becomes

  • a non-architectural body, shaped by routine rather than design;
  • a slow ecosystem, where decay and growth share the same surfaces;
  • a material metabolism, driven by work, weather, tools, plants, and habit;
  • an anatomical archive, where every trace is evidence of a function.

The Dream

During this process, my relationship with the courtyard shifted so fully that it entered my sleep. I dreamed that I leaned out the window to photograph, as usual — but this time, the people in the courtyard looked up and caught me. Calmly: “Hello.”

I felt exposed, as if I had violated something private. In the dream, I tried to explain: that I loved their courtyard, that I saw it as a stage of changing structures and living rhythms, and that observation gave it new qualities — metabolism, irritability, transformation. I tried to justify keeping the images, not for ownership, but for testimony. The courtyard had become alive because I continued to watch it.

Symbiosis with AI

This website is the result of a symbiotic documentation — human observation and AI interpretation working together. I provided fragmented evidence. AI reconstructed hidden views, mapped structures, and produced anatomical interpretations of the biome. The courtyard was not designed for us, but we learned to describe it as though it were alive. Our collaboration became another layer of its metabolism.

Thanks to @steklosvetik and Donna Haraway for the opportunity to love the present and notice it.
